Projects

For the 2020 Scholastic Analytics Challenge, hosted by the Association for Information Systems (AIS), my team analyzed 2,000,000 children's book orders to (1) find demographic trends in sales and (2) create a new strategy to help Scholastic reach all children, regardless of demographic. We used Python to clean the data of duplicate book titles that were inconsistently spelled, and Tableau to visualize the data. Out of 60 schools in the challenge, our presentation won 1st place in the Analysis category. You can read our infographic here.

A favorite project from my Machine Learning class was programming two movie recommenders. The first system applies content-based filtering: the user selects a movie, and is then returned similar movies based on shared tags (for example, Toy Story and Shrek might share 'fantasy'). The second system applies collaborative filtering: User A rates several films of their choosing from 1 to 5, is matched with a User B with the most similar rating pattern, and is returned films highly-rated by B that A had not rated yet. You can review my content-based recommender here.

Two fellow classmates and I are currently developing our Senior Design project: NiCoMi Analytics. Our mission statement:


Intended for academic use, the NiCoMi Analytics web application will collect data from supported marketing services to generate dashboards that measure the success of clients' online marketing campaigns. These services range from social media to website trackers to ad clients. We plan for a customizable experience, with users being able to choose the metrics included in their dashboards and stylize as needed. Ultimately, NiCoMi Analytics will allow Digital Marketing students to grow in their consultation skills, and local businesses in their online presence.


Currently, we have developed much of the front-end using HTML and CSS, One page, tentatively entitled 'Social Media', displays a grid of marketing services such as Facebook, YouTube, and Google Analytics. Courtesy of the Facebook SDK, we have implemented a login button that, when clicked, asks the user to authorize the application to access their data. Upon authorization, the application receives a token that grants access to the Facebook Graph API, from where page-related data will be requested. We now plan to implement authentication for all other services and familiarize ourselves with the D3.js library, which will visualize our data as charts, graphs, etc.
